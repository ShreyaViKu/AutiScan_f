AUTISM SPECTRUM DISORDER

Understanding Autism

A lifelong neurodevelopmental condition that influences how individuals communicate, interact, and experience the world.

What Is Autism?

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by differences in social communication and patterns of behavior, interests, or sensory processing. It begins in early childhood and continues throughout life.

The term "spectrum" reflects the wide range of experiences among autistic individuals. Each person presents a unique combination of strengths, differences, and support needs.

According to current estimates from the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), autism affects approximately 1 in 31 children and 1 in 45 adults in the United States.

The Spectrum of Presentation

Autism does not look the same in every individual. Some people are highly verbal, while others may communicate using alternative or augmentative methods.

Intellectual ability varies widely. Some autistic individuals require significant daily support, while others live independently and pursue advanced academic or professional paths.

Support needs are categorized in the DSM-5-TR into three levels based on functional support requirements.

DSM-5-TR Support Levels

  • Level 1: Requiring support
  • Level 2: Requiring substantial support
  • Level 3: Requiring very substantial support

Co-Occurring Conditions

Many autistic individuals experience additional medical, behavioral, or mental health conditions that may influence daily functioning and quality of life.

Attention-Deficit / Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
Anxiety disorders and depression
Gastrointestinal conditions
Seizure disorders
Sleep disturbances
Sensory processing differences

Diagnosis and Lifespan

Signs of autism often emerge before age three. In the United States, the average age of formal diagnosis is approximately five years.

Autism is lifelong. However, an individual's strengths, challenges, and support needs may evolve across different life stages. Early identification and evidence-based interventions are associated with improved communication skills and long-term outcomes.

Gender Differences and Underdiagnosis

Autism is diagnosed more frequently in boys than girls, yet research suggests that girls and women may present differently. They often demonstrate subtler social differences and may engage in masking behaviors that conceal symptoms.

Masking can delay diagnosis and contribute to mental health challenges, particularly during adolescence and adulthood.
