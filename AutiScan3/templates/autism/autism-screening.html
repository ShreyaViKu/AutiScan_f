What is Autism?

Autism, or Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), is a developmental condition that affects how a child communicates, interacts with others, and experiences the world around them.

According to the American Psychiatric Association and the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5), autism typically appears in early childhood and reflects differences in brain development.

It is not caused by parenting, and it is not an illness that needs to be cured. It simply means a child's brain processes information in a unique way.

How Might Autism Look in a Child?

Every child is different, but some common signs may include:

  • Limited eye contact
  • Delayed speech or difficulty expressing needs
  • Preferring to play alone
  • Repetitive movements such as rocking or hand-flapping
  • Strong attachment to routines
  • Sensitivity to sounds, lights, or textures

Some children may require additional daily support, while others grow up to live independently. That is why it is called a spectrum — it looks different in every child.

A Reassuring Note for Parents

Autism is not the result of something you did or didn't do.

With early understanding, structured support, and appropriate therapies, children with autism can learn, grow, and thrive in their own beautiful way.

Why Early Screening Matters

Early screening for ASD allows children to receive support during critical stages of brain development. According to the CDC and WHO, early identification improves communication, social development, learning ability, and long-term outcomes.

Benefits of Early Screening

Earlier access to speech and behavioral therapy
Improved language and cognitive development
Reduced family stress through early guidance
Stronger academic and life outcomes

Early screening does not label a child — it opens the door to timely support.
