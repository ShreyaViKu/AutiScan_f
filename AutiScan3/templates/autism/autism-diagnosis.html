THE DIAGNOSTIC PROCESS

Autism Diagnosis

Understanding how autism is diagnosed helps families navigate the process with confidence.

How Is Autism Diagnosed?

There is no single medical test for autism. Diagnosis is based on behavioral observation, developmental history, and clinical evaluation. It typically involves a multidisciplinary team including pediatricians, psychologists, and speech-language pathologists.

Diagnosis is guided by criteria in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5), which outlines the core features of autism across social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviors.

The Diagnostic Process

01

Developmental Screening

Initial screening by a pediatrician, often using standardized tools like the M-CHAT-R.

02

Comprehensive Evaluation

In-depth assessment by specialists including observation, cognitive testing, and parent interviews.

03

Diagnosis & Support Plan

If diagnosed, a support plan is developed to guide therapy, education, and family resources.

Who Can Diagnose Autism?

Developmental pediatricians
Child psychiatrists
Clinical psychologists
Pediatric neurologists

Diagnosis Across the Lifespan

While autism is most often diagnosed in childhood, many individuals — particularly women and those with milder support needs — are not diagnosed until adolescence or adulthood.

A late diagnosis can bring clarity and access to support. It is never too late to seek evaluation if you suspect autism in yourself or a family member.

After Diagnosis: What Next?

A diagnosis is not an endpoint — it is the beginning of a pathway to understanding and support. Early intervention, therapy, community resources, and family education all contribute to better outcomes.
